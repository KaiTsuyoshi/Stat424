My (paternal) grandparents had 14 children, 6 of whom are twins (3 pairs). My grandpa was a doctor, and he hoped to use his medical prowess to try and cure his children of the baldness that plagues the Fagan family. So he developed a new drug called Bald Blocker that he believed would prevent people from going bald. However, he was unable to get FDA approval for it, so he decided to conduct the trial on his own children. For each twin in each pair of twins, he randomly assigned one to receive Bald Blocker, and the other to receive a placebo. Then, on those children's 40th birthdays, he rated their hair from 1-10.

Let $X$ be a r.v. that denotes the rating of Fagan's hair on their 40th birthday when taking placebo, and $Y$ when taking Bald Blocker. Then my grandpa obtained 3 realizations of each, $(X_i, Y_i)$ for $i = 1,2,3$.

2a

Describe a way to test $H_0: \mu_Y - \mu_X = 0$. What are the assumptions of this test?

\[ \frac{Y. - X.}{S_p\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}} \sim T_4 \]

We either need a large enough sample size for CLT to hold, or we need the residuals to be normal. The sample size assumption is the weaker of the two. We also need homoscedasticity of the residuals. We also need the $Xs$ and $Ys$ to be independent within groups, and between groups (which they aren't).

2b

Suppose the true difference $\mu_Y - \mu_X = 2$, and that, in each group, the standard deviation is $1.3$. Find the power of this test at level $\alpha = 0.2$.

Using a normal approximation: 0.727

2c

Let $D$ be a random variable that denotes the difference in hair rating between 2 Fagan twins, one of whom received placebo, and the other of whom received Bald Blocker. In other words, $D_i = Y_i - X_i$. Using the same assumptions as stated in 2b, find the mean and sd of $D$, and use it to find the power for a hypothesis test of $H_0: \mu_D = 0$.

Using a normal approximation: 0.727

2d

Using a normal approximation (which you might be able to do on the exam), notice that we get the same results. If we consider the fact that twins will have similar hair (i.e. they are positively correlated), then the variance of $D$ should be less than $2\sigma^2$. Suppose the standard deviation of $D$ is 0.9, and re-estimate.

5b

Suppose the clinician has absolutely no idea how to answer any of your questions, and just intends to use R to automatically create the intervals of the type you recommend. You are too busy to follow up after the collection of the data. What would be your best guess as to which of the methods she should employ? Why?

Tukey is built for pairwise comparisons, and there is a large enough number of groups that it is more than likely better than Bonferroni. Scheffe is probably too conservative.

5c

Suppose the clinician wants to be able to add new confidence intervals on top of all pairwise differences as she writes her paper. What method would you suggest? Why?

Scheffe. As she goes on she wouldn't need to readjust past CIs. Additionally, it's likely that with this number of groups (105+), it would be better than Bonferroni.

6a

My dog, Milo, is a fan of many foods. Some of his favorites are blueberries, pickles, sweet potato, and chicken. I want to see which of these is his favorite. Suppose an accurate measure of how much he enjoys a food is how quickly he eats it, measured in seconds/gram. Describe an experiment (procedure, model, and test) to try to detect differences in preference among the 4.

On each day, I collect one sample without replacement from a hat containing 4 of each group. Then, I give him that treat after our morning walk, and record the time it takes to eat it.

Model: $Y_{ik} = \mu + F_i + \varepsilon_{ij}$

Test: Typical F-test

Suppose we want to construct simultanoeus 90% CIs to compare each of the 4 foods with eachother and with each 50/50 mixture of the foods (pickle vs sweet potato, pickle vs half sweet potato half chicken, half blueberry half chicken vs half sweet potato half pickle, ... ).
